I've verified that the power supply is working correctly and I get 5V at the supply terminals. When I measure the voltage across the resistor, I get 5V. When I measure the voltage across the LED, I get 0V. The connections look correct visually.
Diagnosis: Okay, let's break this down step-by-step using your provided measurements and the context.

**Circuit Configuration:**
You have a simple series circuit: `5V Supply` -> `330 Ohm Resistor` -> `Red LED` -> `Ground`.

**Your Measurements and Observations:**
1.  **Power Supply (V_supply):** 5V (verified correct).
2.  **LED State:** Not lighting up.
3.  **Voltage across Resistor (V_R):** 5V.
4.  **Voltage across LED (V_LED):** 0V.
5.  **Visual Inspection:** Connections look correct.

---

### Step-by-Step Analysis:

The key to diagnosing this lies in reconciling the voltage measurements.

1.  **LED Not Lighting & V_LED = 0V:**
    *   If the LED is not lighting up and you measure 0V across its terminals, it implies one of two things:
        *   **No current is flowing through the LED (I = 0A):** If there's no potential difference, there's no current, or the current is too low to cause a voltage drop.
        *   **The LED is short-circuited:** If the LED itself acts as a short, it would have 0V across it, and no light would be emitted from the LED junction.

2.  **Voltage Across Resistor (V_R) = 5V:**
    *   For a resistor to have a 5V drop across it, there *must* be current flowing through it. Using Ohm's Law (I = V/R): I = 5V / 330 Ohms = 0.01515 A (approx. 15.15 mA).
    *   This current (15.15 mA) is a typical and appropriate current for a red LED to light up brightly.
    *   If 5V is dropped across the resistor in a 5V supply circuit, it means one end of the resistor is at 5V (connected to the supply) and the other end is at 0V (relative to ground).

3.  **Reconciling Measurements:**
    *   If 15.15 mA is flowing through the resistor (because V_R = 5V), then the same 15.15 mA *must also be flowing through the LED* since they are in series.
    *   However, if 15.15 mA is flowing through the LED, it *should* light up and have a forward voltage drop (Vf) of approximately 1.8V to 2.2V for a red LED.
    *   But your measurement shows **V_LED = 0V** and the LED is **not lighting up**.

    The only way for all these measurements to be true simultaneously is if **the LED is acting as a short circuit.**

    *   **If the LED is shorted:**
        *   It will have 0V across it (V_LED = 0V).
        *   It will not light up (because the current bypasses the light-emitting junction, or the junction itself is destroyed).
        *   Current will flow through the resistor, through the shorted LED, to ground.
        *   The resistor will effectively be connected directly from 5V to ground (via the short). Thus, the full 5V supply will drop across the 330 ohm resistor (V_R = 5V). This fits perfectly.

---

### Possible Causes:

Based on the consistent interpretation of your measurements, the primary cause is related to the LED itself or a short across its terminals.

1.  **Shorted LED:** The LED has failed internally in a short-circuit condition. This is the most direct explanation for all observations.
2.  **Short Circuit Across LED Terminals:** There might be an accidental short circuit bypassing the LED, such as:
    *   A stray strand of wire.
    *   An inadvertent solder bridge on your breadboard or PCB.
    *   The LED leads themselves touching where they shouldn't.
    *   Incorrect wiring causing the LED to be bypassed.
3.  **Incorrect LED Polarity (Combined with Short):** While a simply reverse-biased LED would typically act as an open circuit (or block current until a high reverse breakdown voltage is reached, which would then damage it), it's possible for a severely damaged or incorrectly wired LED to also exhibit a short when reverse-biased. However, a simple short is more likely.

---

### Suggested Tests:

To confirm the diagnosis, perform these tests in order:

1.  **Visually Inspect the LED and its Connections Closely:**
    *   Carefully check for any stray wire strands, solder bridges, or bent leads that might be shorting the LED's terminals.
    *   Double-check that the LED is installed with the correct polarity (anode to resistor side, cathode to ground side). The longer lead is typically the anode (+), and the shorter lead/flat edge on the casing is the cathode (-).

2.  **Test the LED Out of Circuit (Most Critical Test):**
    *   **Remove the LED completely from your circuit.**
    *   **Multimeter Diode Test Mode:**
        *   Set your multimeter to diode test mode.
        *   Place the red probe on the anode and the black probe on the cathode. A working LED should show a voltage drop (typically 1.5-2.2V for a red LED) and emit a faint light (or clearly light up if your multimeter supplies enough current).
        *   Reverse the probes. It should show an "OL" (open circuit) or very high resistance.
        *   **If the LED is shorted, it will show a very low resistance (near 0 Ohms) or a "beep" in continuity mode in *both* directions.** This would confirm the diagnosis.
    *   **Multimeter Resistance Mode (Ohm Meter):**
        *   Measure resistance in both directions. A shorted LED will show very low resistance in both directions.

3.  **Test for Short Across LED's Position (In-Circuit, Power Off):**
    *   With the power supply disconnected and the LED removed from the circuit, use your multimeter in **continuity mode** (or low resistance mode).
    *   Place your probes on the two points in the circuit where the LED was connected (the end of the resistor and the ground connection).
    *   If you hear a beep or measure very low resistance (near 0 Ohms), there's a short circuit in your wiring or breadboard at the LED's position, bypassing where the LED should be.

4.  **Test the Resistor (Optional, if previous tests don't reveal issue):**
    *   Remove the resistor from the circuit.
    *   Measure its resistance with a multimeter. It should read approximately 330 Ohms. (This is less likely to be the issue given your current measurements).

5.  **Try a Known Good LED:**
    *   If you have another red LED, insert it into the circuit. If the new LED lights up correctly, your original LED was faulty (likely shorted).
