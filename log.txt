Input: I built a simple LED circuit using a 9V battery and a 330Ω resistor built in series, but the LED is not turning on. The battery is new. What could be wrong and how can I test it?
Diagnosis: This is a classic problem in basic electronics, and the provided context perfectly frames the approach. Let's break it down.

---

### Possible Causes:

1.  **Incorrect LED Orientation (Polarity):** LEDs are diodes and are polarized components. Current must flow from the anode (+) to the cathode (-). If connected backward, the LED will block current (act as an an open circuit) and not light up.
    *   The **anode** is typically the longer leg.
    *   The **cathode** is typically the shorter leg, and sometimes indicated by a flat edge on the LED body.
2.  **Faulty Connections / Bad Wiring:**
    *   **Loose Contacts:** Wires not making good contact in a breadboard, alligator clips, or at battery terminals.
    *   **Broken Wire:** A wire might be internally broken, even if it looks fine from the outside.
    *   **Poor Solder Joint:** If any soldering was involved, a cold joint could be the culprit.
3.  **Faulty LED:**
    *   The LED itself could be burned out, internally open-circuited, or short-circuited due to a manufacturing defect or previous overcurrent.
4.  **Faulty Resistor:**
    *   Though less common, the resistor could be internally open-circuited, preventing any current flow.
5.  **Dead or Weak Battery:**
    *   Even if "new," the battery could be faulty, discharged from shelf life, or have poor internal connections.
    *   Poor contact between the battery terminals and your circuit connectors.

---

### Suggested Tests:

You will need a **multimeter** for most of these tests, which is an invaluable tool for troubleshooting.

1.  **Check LED Orientation:**
    *   **Test:** Carefully inspect your LED. The **longer leg is the anode (+)**, and the **shorter leg is the cathode (-)**. Ensure the anode is connected towards the positive side of your battery (through the resistor) and the cathode towards the negative side.
    *   **Expected Result:** Correcting the orientation makes the LED light up.

2.  **Check Battery Voltage:**
    *   **Test:** Set your multimeter to **DC Voltage mode** (e.g., 20V range). Place the red probe on the positive terminal of the 9V battery and the black probe on the negative terminal.
    *   **Expected Result:** The reading should be close to 9V (e.g., 8.5V to 9.5V).
    *   **If Result is Low/Zero:** The battery is dead or faulty. Replace it.

3.  **Check for Continuity / Bad Connections:**
    *   **Test 1 (Visual & Physical):** Carefully inspect all connections in your circuit. Wiggle wires slightly. Ensure they are fully inserted into breadboard holes or making firm contact with clips. Sometimes a loose connection is intermittent.
    *   **Test 2 (Multimeter Continuity Test):** Set your multimeter to **Continuity mode** (often indicated by a diode symbol or a speaker icon that beeps).
        *   **Check Wires:** Touch the probes to each end of every wire in your circuit. It should beep or show very low resistance (near 0 Ohms). If it shows "OL" (Open Loop) or infinite resistance, the wire is broken.
        *   **Check Battery Terminals:** Ensure your connectors are making firm electrical contact with the battery's terminals.

4.  **Test the Resistor:**
    *   **Test:** Disconnect the 330Ω resistor from the circuit. Set your multimeter to **Resistance mode (Ohms)** (e.g., 2kΩ range). Place the probes across the two leads of the resistor.
    *   **Expected Result:** The reading should be very close to 330Ω (e.g., 320Ω - 340Ω).
    *   **If Result is "OL" or a very high, unstable reading:** The resistor is open-circuited and faulty. Replace it.

5.  **Test the LED (Diode Test):**
    *   **Test:** Disconnect the LED from the circuit. Set your multimeter to **Diode Test mode** (often shared with continuity mode, look for a diode symbol).
        *   Place the **red probe on the anode (longer leg)** and the **black probe on the cathode (shorter leg)**.
        *   **Expected Result (Forward Bias):** The multimeter should display a forward voltage drop (typically 1.5V to 3V for common LEDs), and the LED might even light up dimly.
        *   Now, **reverse the probes**: Place the black probe on the anode and the red probe on the cathode.
        *   **Expected Result (Reverse Bias):** The multimeter should display "OL" (Open Loop) or infinite resistance, as a good diode blocks current in reverse.
    *   **If LED fails either of these tests (e.g., displays "OL" in both directions, or a very low resistance in both directions, or doesn't show voltage drop/light up in forward bias):** The LED is faulty. Replace it.

By systematically performing these tests, you should be able to identify the specific component or connection that is preventing your LED from lighting up.
